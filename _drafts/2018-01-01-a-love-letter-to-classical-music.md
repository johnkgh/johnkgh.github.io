The best music has already been written

Back in college, I tried to convice my friend to open his mind and consider songs out of his listening repertoire. He didn't do the same, but i ended up sucummbing to his tastes instead. By the end of this "experiment", I thought that the music he listened to was objectively better and more palatable to the ear.

And I still think he's great (although I haven't listened to a song after TPAB)

I'm often reticent about answering the question "What kind of music do you listen to?". Because whenever I answer classical or jazz, it's usually met with scoffs.

I've often heard that "all classical music sounds the same". Which is true in a sense, because the instrumentation is similar from period to period, from century to century. I think it's a fair criticism.

However, I think people nowawadays are more concerned with *how* a song sounds versus the composition of a song. While the instruments are the same for classical music, there is really no box for which the music must be confined into. There is no major/minor 4/4 verse/chorus/bridge structure that the pieces live under. The composer is free to step outside the bounds of the period he lives in. that's why we can have such varying pieces like: and:

People care too much about the type of drum, the reverb, etc, but there's no *juice*.

This problem of structure is not only endemic to popular music*

* when I say popular music, I don't just mean pure pop, but pretty much the umbrella of music that isn't classical i.e. with voice, drums, guitar, and the major/minor 4/4 verse/chorus/bridge structure that we talked about earlier.



I have pored through hours and hours of shit music just to find a small nugget of gold in this huge and increasing pile of shit.


This is more of an observation with music in general, but it's ephemeral. While the visual arts valued by worth equating how much pieces cost, or whether they're originals, music is less concerned with these matters*. Music is ephemeral; it can't exist without the medium of time. Sure, one might reduce it to a series of soundwaves and reverberations, but it never really exists in the concrete sense. It is more of an abstract quality, some idea of sounds harmonizing and coalescing together.

And here is where I can argue that classical music embodies much more of a platonic ideal than most of it's contemporary counterparts. The sheet music is merely just an idea of how something should sound.

With producers using studios, what is released *is* the work. It was his or her most final work, and best that the artist could do, and it usually falls flat.

While in classical music, there was some platonic idea that the composer was attempting to put forth, and the sheet music was merely a limiting way to capture its essence. It lends the interpreter, being a solo performer or an orchestra, to interpret this work to the best of their ability. Maybe this is an inherent flaw, but I think it lends itself to beauty.
